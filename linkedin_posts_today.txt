1. COLLABORATIVE ARTICLE
==================================================
Imagine a founder hitting 3 million dollars in annual revenue. On paper, the business looks like a success. In reality, the bank account feels like a lie. Every time a large customer deposit hits, there is a sudden rush of adrenaline. But that feeling vanishes quickly. The money disappears into a void of operational costs, tax obligations, and payments to the subcontractors who actually do the work. This is the trap of the subcontractor heavy service model. When customers pay upfront and subcontractors get paid on 30 day terms, the business creates a fake sense of wealth. The cash is there, but it isn't actually yours. It belongs to the people doing the labor and the government. 

Most founders make the mistake of treating their main account as a single pool of money. They see 50,000 dollars and think they are rich, only to realize 30,000 belongs to the crew and 10,000 is for the tax man. This is where the confusion between accounting and cash allocation happens. Accounting tells you if you made a profit over a month. Cash allocation tells you exactly who owns the money in your bank account today. The breakthrough happens when the founder stops looking at one big number and starts splitting every single deposit the second it arrives. 

The fix is simple. Divide every payment into four distinct buckets. Put 60 percent into a subcontractor account. Put 20 percent into a tax account. Put 10 percent into operational costs. The remaining 10 percent goes to profit. By moving the money immediately, the founder no longer guesses if they can afford a new hire or a piece of equipment. They see the actual profit bucket and know that money is safe to spend. The anxiety of the net 30 payment cycle disappears because the money is already waiting in a separate account. It turns a chaotic cash flow struggle into a predictable system. The founder finally sleeps knowing the bills are covered and the profit is real.

How do you manage your deposits to avoid the feeling of being broke while growing?
